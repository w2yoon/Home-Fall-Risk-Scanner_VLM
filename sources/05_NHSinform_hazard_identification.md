## Full text

Over half of all falls happen at home, where we spend much of our time and tend to move around without thinking about our safety. Falls prevention in and around the home is often described as 'common sense', however, it isn't always easy to recognise the things that can cause trips, slips and falls.

Although hazards within the home often contribute to a fall, more often than not falls are caused by personal risk factors.

### What can I do?

Many falls could be prevented by making a few small changes in your home:
- remove or avoid trip and slip hazards
- improve lighting
- store frequently used items within easier reach
- change or adapt furniture and furnishings to give more support

It can be helpful to have someone else — a friend, relative, or neighbour — look with you to see if there's anything that could be made safer.

### Trip and slip hazards

- clear clutter from your floors or stairs — including small furniture, pet bowls, trailing cables, or other things that can cause you to trip
- avoid leaving or storing items on stairs and hallways
- arrange furniture to give you plenty of room to walk freely
- remove loose mats and rugs to reduce the risk of tripping accidentally

Loose floor coverings and changes in flooring type from one room to the next can be a trip hazard. People with a visual impairment (e.g., Parkinson's disease or dementia) are at higher risk from uneven flooring.

To improve flooring safety:
- secure carpets, and remove mats and rugs
- replace carpet with a self-coloured one (better for vision problems)
- lower high thresholds in doorways

### Slippery surfaces

Laminate and tiled floors can be slippery — avoid walking in stocking soles. Clean up spills right away. Some air freshener sprays can leave a film on carpets or flooring, making them slippery.

### Lighting

Poor lighting (dull lighting, shadows/dark areas, or glare) can cause trips, especially with poor eyesight.

- always have good lighting on stairways and hallways
- have a lamp or light within easy reach of your bed, and a torch by your bed for power outages
- consider night lights in the bathroom, hallways, and bedroom (as a guide to the nearest switch, not a replacement for switching on a light)
- put a light on for nighttime toilet trips

**Changing light bulbs**: if unsteady, dizzy, or lightheaded, changing a bulb is a fall risk — ask for help, or use a step stool with a handle if doing it yourself.

### Furniture and furnishings

- chairs and beds that are too low make it difficult to get up and steady yourself
- soft mattresses, pillows, and cushions can also make this harder
- **furniture with wheels/casters lacks stability and can move unexpectedly when you sit or lie down**

Recommendations:
- remove casters from the bottom of chairs, beds, and tables so they become more stable
- move tables against walls and away from walkways
- buy firmer pillows/cushions, or double up for more support

### Colour and contrast

People with visual impairment (Parkinson's, stroke, dementia) are at higher risk. Use contrasting colours on a chair seat, bed cover, and toilet to help identify where to sit — e.g., a dark bed/seat cover against a light carpet.

### Storing and carrying items

Over-reaching, bending down low, or standing on a stool takes excellent balance. Store often-used items within easy reach and comfortable height. If walking and carrying items together is difficult, ask an occupational therapist about a trolley or walking-frame caddy.

### How an occupational therapist can help

- checking how you manage daily activities
- arranging equipment (rails, walking aids)
- helping find better ways to carry out everyday tasks

Referral is via local social work department or health centre.
